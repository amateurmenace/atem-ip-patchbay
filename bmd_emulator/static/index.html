Turn multiple worldwide video sources, from iPhones to Drones to NDI sources to computer screens, into remote inputs that stream directly into your ATEM Switcher or Blackmagic Streaming Decoders / Bridges over the single ethernet cable. Even route Dante audio onto a video source that maps directly to one of your switcher's SDI or HDMI inputs!
What it does

If you have an ATEM Mini Extreme ISO G2, Television Studio HD8 ISO, or an upcoming qualifying ST2110 ATEM Switcher, you can change a local input into a remote input that can be sent over the public Internet directly to your switcher from anywhere in the world. Blackmagic Streaming Decoder and Streaming Bridges can also receive sources from anywhere with a stable enough internet connection (~2.5–3.5 Mbps upload), but previously this was limited to just other Blackmagic hardware. Now, NDI, SDI, HDMI, non-Blackmagic SRT and RTMP streams, etc. can all be converted into the special Blackmagic flavor of SRT using this app.

How it happened

Made possible in part by the latest update to the iOS Blackmagic Camera App, which for the first time let that excellent free app stream directly into an input on a qualifying ATEM. Because it's software and not Blackmagic hardware, they seem to have lessened some of the handshake requirements between sender and receiver, so it was much easier to deconstruct what was essential and what was just noise. Then Claude and I (mostly Claude) built an emulator of a streaming encoder and eventually stumbled onto the winning combination that got a signal into the ATEM Mini Extreme ISO G2 that inspired this journey.

This is more of a demo app showing what is now possible, a proof of concept, and should only be used in real productions at your own risk. It is free forever, until it either gets stopped by Blackmagic or they fully embrace opening up their powerful stream decoding ecosystem.

Recovery

      

        If the Stop button isn't ending a stream, the
        FFmpeg subprocess that's actually pushing the bytes has been
        orphaned — the parent app crashed, was force-quit, or got
        rebuilt during development, and the FFmpeg child kept running
        in its own process group. The stream keeps going to your ATEM
        until you kill the orphan or until the OS reaps it (which
        could be a long time).
      

      

        This button finds and kills any leftover FFmpeg streams
        spawned by this app (matched by the BMD-flavored
        streamid= pattern in the FFmpeg cmdline, so
        unrelated FFmpeg jobs you have running stay untouched). It
        sends SIGTERM first so the encoder flushes its
        buffer + closes the SRT/RTMP connection cleanly, then
        SIGKILL after a short settle for anything that
        ignored the polite ask.
      

      
      

        Safe to run anytime — won't touch unrelated FFmpeg jobs.
        After clicking, your ATEM may still hold the receiver slot
        for ~30s-2min before accepting a new connection on the same
        stream key (this is a separate ATEM-side lockout, not
        something the app can short-circuit).

            How does port forwarding work?
            

              
Wherever your ATEM lives — a home studio, a corporate
              office, a venue's IT closet, a church multipurpose room —
              the stream has to traverse the router or firewall sitting
              in front of it. By default these devices
              block unsolicited inbound traffic, so
              somebody has to tell the device to forward SRT/RTMP
              packets to your ATEM. Who that "somebody" is depends on
              who controls the network.


              
If you control the router (home / small office)

              
    
                
  1. Open the router's admin page (commonly
                  192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1) —
                  credentials are usually printed on the bottom of the
                  unit.
    2. 
                
  2. Find Port Forwarding (sometimes
                  labeled "NAT", "Virtual Server", or "Advanced").
    3. 
                
  3. Add a rule: forward
                  UDP port 1935 for SRT (or
                  TCP port 1935 for RTMP) to your
                  ATEM's local IP (e.g. 192.168.1.50).
    4. 
                
  4. From the remote site, point this app at your home's
                  public IP (look it up via
                  api.ipify.org),
                  not the ATEM's local IP.
    5. 
              


              
If IT / facilities controls the network (work, venue, church, school)

              
Open a ticket or email IT/AV with everything they need
              to act on it in one go — guess the answer wrong and
              you'll bounce twice for clarification:

              
    
                
  • What: "Inbound port forward,
                  external UDP 1935 (SRT) — or
                  TCP 1935 if we're using RTMP — to the
                  ATEM at internal IP X.X.X.X, port
                  1935."
    • 
                
  • Why: "We're sending live video to
                  the ATEM from outside the building (remote camera,
                  off-site contributor, stage rig at another address).
                  Without the forward, the connection lands at the
                  edge router and never reaches the ATEM."
    • 
                
  • Risk profile: "Single port,
                  forwarded to one device — the ATEM only listens for
                  the BMD-flavored SRT/RTMP handshake; no shell, no
                  filesystem, no general-purpose service exposed."
    • 
                
  • What we need from you: the
                  public IP or hostname we should
                  point our encoder at, and confirmation when the
                  rule is in place. SRT also needs the firewall to
                  allow outbound UDP on the same port if it
                  filters egress.
    • 
                
  • If they push back: ask whether
                  they can give the ATEM a static internal IP (so the
                  rule stays valid across reboots), and whether they
                  have an existing pinhole / DMZ pattern they prefer
                  for AV gear — most enterprise networks already have
                  one for video conferencing systems.
    • 
              


              
Some networks (mobile carrier hotspots,
              shared apartment building connections, certain hotel /
              coffee-shop wifi, schools behind a single content
              filter) use carrier-grade NAT which
              silently blocks all inbound traffic regardless of any
              port-forward rule. Test by pushing from a phone on
              cellular data: if the stream reaches your ATEM,
              forwarding works; if not, you'll need an SRT relay
              (a small VPS, Cloudflare TURN, OvenMediaEngine, or this
              app's own SRT receiver mode running on a publicly-
              reachable host) to bridge the gap.

                How do I find my switcher's format?
                

                  
ATEM Software Control: open the app →
                  Settings (gear icon, top-right) → Video →
                  Set video standard. Whatever's checked there is what
                  you pick here.

                  
From the unit itself: ATEM Mini Extreme G2,
                  Television Studio HD, and similar models display the current
                  standard on the front-panel screen — look for
                  1080p30 / 1080p59.94 / 1080p25 / etc.

                  
Common defaults:
                  1080p30 in NTSC regions (US, Canada, Japan),
                  1080p25 in PAL regions (most of Europe, Asia,
                  Australia), 1080p59.94 for broadcast-spec setups.

                  
If you set the wrong format here, the only
                  symptom is "stream connects but ATEM input shows black /
                  frozen / corrupt." Try cycling formats one by one until the
                  ATEM accepts.

          Using Dante?
          

            

              Dante Virtual Soundcard
              (DVSC) lets a regular Mac receive any Dante stream on your AVB / AES67 network and expose
              the receive channels as a CoreAudio device — which then shows up in this card's Audio
              device dropdown when you switch to Custom mode. To set it up the first time:
            

            
    
              
  1. Install Dante Virtual Soundcard from Audinate (paid licence —
                a perpetual key is around $50). Reboot when it asks you to.
    2. 
              
  2. Open Dante Virtual Soundcard from your menu bar →
                set Audio Interface to Core Audio,
                Channels to whatever your job needs (2 / 8 / 16 / 32 / 64 — match
                your console / stage box), Sample Rate to 48 kHz
                (this app's encoder is locked to 48 kHz), then click Start.
    3. 
              
  3. Open Dante Controller
                and route channels from the Dante transmitter (your console / stage box / DAW) to
                this Mac's receive channels. The L/R picker that appears once you pick a
                multi-channel device is this Mac's receive-channel numbers, exactly what
                you see in Dante Controller's grid for this machine.
    4. 
              
  4. Switch this card to Custom audio source above, then pick
                "Dante Virtual Soundcard" from the Audio device dropdown. The
                Channels (L · R) picker will appear — set it to whichever pair
                you want on the outgoing stream (e.g. 5 · 6 for stage L/R).
    5. 
            

            

              Aggregate Devices (Audio MIDI Setup → "+" → Create Aggregate Device)
              also work — anything multi-channel that shows up to CoreAudio gets the same picker.
              Dante AVIO USB adapters work too, since they also expose receive channels via CoreAudio.
              FFmpeg pulls the selected pair via -af pan="stereo|c0=cN|c1=cM"; without
              this picker, FFmpeg auto-downmixes all N channels (front + rear + center +
              LFE all summed), which sounds wrong for live audio routing.

Latency budget

        
    
          
  • SRT push — 200–500 ms typical (we set latency=500000 µs by default)
    • 
          
  • Encoder — 50–100 ms (libx265/x264, preset veryfast tune zerolatency)
    • 
          
  • Total end-to-end — 250–600 ms typical
    • 
          
  • Bandwidth — 2.5 Mbps (Low) · 4.5 Mbps (Medium) · 6 Mbps (High); 1080p60 needs ~1.5× more
    • 
          
  • Avoid: 5+ second buffering when SRT latency is set too high

FAQ

        

          Stream connects but ATEM input shows black
          
Format mismatch. The switcher's video standard (1080p30, 1080p59.94, etc.) must match
          what we're sending exactly. Pick the right value in the Switcher video format
          dropdown above the Address field.

        

        

          What's the minimum upload bandwidth I need?
          
2.5 Mbps for the Low quality preset, 4.5 for Medium, 6 for High. 1080p60 needs roughly
          1.5× those numbers. Don't push more than ~70% of your measured upload speed — the rest
          covers protocol overhead and bursty I-frames.

        

        

          Can I use this with a non-ATEM Blackmagic device?
          
Yes. Web Presenter HD/4K, Streaming Bridge, and Streaming Decoder all accept the same
          BMD-flavored SRT handshake. Web Presenters consume RTMP too, which is in the Protocol
          chooser above.

        

        

          Is this Blackmagic-approved?
          
No. This is community work. The protocol and XML formats are publicly documented but
          BMD doesn't endorse third-party encoders. Use in real productions at your own risk.

        

        

          Why does my source appear delayed by ~500 ms on the ATEM?
          
That's SRT's latency budget — the receiver buffers half a second so packet retransmits
          have time to land. Reduce SRT latency in the Advanced section if your network
          is fast and stable; raise it on flaky connections.

        

        

          What's the difference between NDI direct and the bridge workflow?
          
Direct NDI (v0.2.0) bypasses NDI Virtual Camera entirely — frames stream from the NDI
          sender straight into our encoder via the NewTek SDK. No menu-bar setup. The old bridge
          workflow (NDI Virtual Camera + AVF) had a known frame=0 hang with FFmpeg.

        

        

          SRT vs RTMP — which should I pick?
          
SRT is the right answer for almost every modern use case.
          Built by Haivision specifically for unreliable internet links, SRT recovers from
          packet loss via in-flight retransmits, optionally encrypts the stream end-to-end
          with AES, and survives jitter that makes RTMP collapse. Real Blackmagic encoders
          (the Web Presenter line, ATEM Streaming Bridge) all default to SRT for the same
          reason. Pick SRT unless you're talking to a destination that genuinely doesn't
          support it.

          
RTMP is the older Adobe-era protocol — TCP-based, no native
          encryption, no automatic recovery. Still required for some specific endpoints
          (legacy Web Presenter HD models, YouTube Live's primary ingest, some older CDN
          setups). The protocol picker above lets you switch in one click; everything else
          (key, codec, bitrate) carries over.

          
If your destination supports both, SRT will hold up over a flaky
          coffee-shop wifi where RTMP would freeze.

        

        

          H.264 vs H.265 — which codec?
          
H.265 (HEVC) is the right default if your destination supports
          it. Roughly 30-50% better compression at the same visual quality, which means you
          can either (a) hit the same quality at lower bandwidth, or (b) use the bandwidth
          you have for noticeably crisper video. ATEM Mini Extreme G2, Television Studio HD8 ISO,
          Streaming Bridge, and Web Presenter 4K all decode H.265 natively.

          
H.264 is universal — every BMD device since the original Web
          Presenter HD plays it, and so does every other RTMP/SRT decoder out there. Pick
          H.264 when the receiving device is older (Web Presenter HD, original Streaming
          Bridge), when you're pushing to a non-Blackmagic endpoint that hasn't added HEVC
          yet, or when you're not sure what's on the other end and want maximum
          compatibility.

          
Both codecs run on the Apple ANE + GPU on macOS (we use
          h264_videotoolbox / hevc_videotoolbox) so encoder CPU
          cost is in the single digits either way — pick based on the destination, not on
          your laptop's load.

        

        

          Why does the encoder feel so light on CPU compared to OBS?
          
On macOS we route encoding through Apple's hardware video pipeline (the ANE +
          GPU) via FFmpeg's videotoolbox encoders. OBS uses libx264/libx265 on CPU by
          default, which costs ~80% of one core at 1080p30. We sit in the single digits
          for the same output quality, freeing headroom for NDI receive, mux, and SRT
          pacing. On Windows, the equivalent would be h264_qsv / nvenc / amf — defer to
          a follow-up.
